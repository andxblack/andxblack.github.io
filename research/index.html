Research

I am part of the Stochastic Modelling and Operations Research Group in the School of Mathematical Sciences. I would tend to describe my research as broadly lying at the intersection of four topics: stochastic modelling, mathematical biology, computational Bayesian statistics and Monte-Carlo methods. Below I describe some of my current interests and projects.

Epidemic modelling and inference

A large part of my research is in epidemic modelling. I am involved in developing new methods for inferring the likely impact of novel pathogens from data collected in the early stages of an outbreak, in particular influenza. Studies that will collect such data are often known as First Few Hundred (FF100) studies because they aim to monitor the first few hundred infected individuals and their household contacts.

I am also interested in using similar methods to characterise the 1918 influenza pandemic from historical maritime data sources.

Computational Bayesian statistics.

Bayesian statistics is the natural framework for tacking inference problems where we have an underlying probabilistic or mechanistic model. Computational techniques and algorithms are an important part of this area as anything apart from the simplest models are intractable in some respect—Markov chain Monte Carlo is the grandaddy of these algorithms.

A particular challenge with inference for epidemic models is that that likelihood itself is often intractable. A fun part of my research has been developing new simulation methods leveraging sequential Monte Carlo, particle filters and importance sampling techniques to efficiently estimate likelihoods instead of calculating them analytically. These methods are currently powering state-of-the-art pandemic preparedness methods.

I am also interested in using machine learning methods to increase the efficiency of Bayesian approaches (which are often computationally intensive) and learning optimal proposals for importance sampling algorithms. Generally I like problems at the interface of computation and probability. I am keen to talk to students with a strong background in these areas about potential HDR projects.

Evolutionary transitions

These are events that are responsible for building the nested hierarchy we see in biology today and the creation of new levels of biological organisation. The best known example of a transition is from unicellular to multicellular life, but there are many others, such as genes into chromosomes and the formation of the first cells. My favourite book on this topic is Darwinian Populations and Natural Selection by Peter Godfrey-Smith.

My interest is in building mathematical models that can capture the dynamics of evolutionary transitions and to understand them from a mechanistic point of view. The challenging part of this is to understand the very earliest stages of the transition, especially how early groups of cells came to possess Darwinian characteristics needed for natural selection to act at the level of groups. In modelling this phenomena, the basic concepts that we normally rely on when doing evolutionary analysis, such as reproduction, now must themselves be explained, rather than being assumed in the model. This research is in collaboration with Paul Rainey and Pierrick Bourrat, and the solution to the problem we advocate is called “Ecological Scaffolding”. This is the idea that Darwinian properties can be scaffolded by the environment, these properties are exogenously imposed and can effect evolutionary transitions. See our new paper for a detailed discussion of this. This shift in perspective changes the problem from an abstract one of understanding levels of selection to a more concrete problem of understanding the ecological conditions that can create scaffolded populations. Currently a PhD student is working on this topic and developing advanced stochastic models to probe these questions.
